This week’s most important lesson is that everyone sees the world through their own “cultural glasses.” Our background shapes how we understand reality and interpret other people’s actions. The idea of the “Cultural Iceberg” shows that most of a culture — its values and beliefs — lies hidden beneath the surface. To reach real cross‑cultural awareness, we need to look beyond surface differences and recognize how identities overlap. Issues like racism, sexism, and xenophobia often reinforce one another, creating complex challenges that can’t be solved by focusing on just one group. Understanding this helps us see identity in a deeper, more connected way and pushes us toward genuine social justice.

As a student, this framework encourages deep inquiry and helps us embrace diversity in ways that support both personal and academic growth. It teaches us to observe, learn, and understand the actions of classmates, shifting our attitudes to benefit from a diverse learning environment instead of reacting with confrontation or criticism toward unfamiliar cultural differences. The “iceberg” model is a powerful tool here. It reminds us to look beyond surface differences like language, food, or clothing and engage with the deeper beliefs, values, and social norms that shape the people around us. By doing so, we gain a richer understanding of others and create a more respectful, inclusive space for learning.

In business, this knowledge offers a practical guide for improving teamwork, spotting system flaws, and strengthening everyday communication. It shows that diverse boards and companies often perform better than monocultural ones, because varied perspectives boost problem‑solving, creativity, and innovation. Using an intersectional lens also helps professionals notice policy gaps that may overlook people facing multiple layers of discrimination. Finally, it reminds us that workplace values like courtesy, hierarchy, and personal space differ across cultures. Recognizing these differences prevents costly misunderstandings and builds a stronger sense of belonging and fairness in multicultural environments.

This course shows how overlapping privilege and injustice shape our lives, and how understanding them can drive real change. Intersectionality gives us the language to see how different forms of privilege and oppression combine, reminding us that people in marginalized groups cannot simply ignore their identity. This awareness builds empathy and pushes us toward stronger social action. By using this perspective, we can better evaluate and reform institutions that may unintentionally harm people with layered identities. Changing our “cultural glasses” also helps us avoid misinterpreting unfamiliar behaviors — like someone’s need for distance or silence — as rejection. Instead, it opens the door to more meaningful, empathetic relationships across our divided world.
