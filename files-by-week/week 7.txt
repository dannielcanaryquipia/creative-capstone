The ongoing crisis of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls (MMIWG) is not a series of isolated tragedies but a systemic issue tied to the intergenerational trauma of colonial policies. The documentary Our Sisters in Spirit connects directly to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission’s Calls to Action. It supports Call to Action #41 by echoing families’ urgent demand for a national inquiry into the disproportionate victimization of Indigenous women and girls. The film also highlights knowledge gaps and government opacity, aligning with Call to Action #39, which calls for a national strategy to collect and share data on victimization. The lack of culturally appropriate support and failures in policing reflect the need for Call to Action #40, urging properly funded, Indigenous‑specific victim services. Finally, the dismissive treatment of families by officials underscores the importance of Call to Action #57, which requires public servants to receive training in intercultural competency, conflict resolution, human rights, and anti‑racism.

The most important lesson from this course is that colonial systems, past and present, deliberately disrupted Indigenous family life and devalued the vital role of women, creating many of the challenges Indigenous Peoples face today. This knowledge is a powerful resource for both personal and professional growth. As students, it gives us a lifelong responsibility to seek accurate history, include Indigenous perspectives, and practice genuine empathy. As professionals, it highlights the moral duty to build cultural competency, consult meaningfully, and ensure workplace policies address structural injustices instead of repeating colonial harm. Finally, as citizens, it calls us to support the 94 Calls to Action, uplift grassroots leadership, and listen to lived experiences. In doing so, we move from passive awareness to active solidarity, helping to build a fair society where no one is left behind.
